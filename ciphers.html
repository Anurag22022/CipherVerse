Caesar Cipher

The Caesar Cipher is one of the simplest and most widely known encryption techniques. It involves shifting each letter of the plaintext by a fixed number of positions in the alphabet.

Example: "HELLO" with a shift of 3 becomes "KHOOR".

Enigma Machine

The Enigma Machine was used by the Germans in WWII, employing multiple rotors to create complex encryption. It was famously cracked by the Allies at Bletchley Park.

Example: Intercepted encrypted messages were cracked, changing the course of the war.

Elliptic Curve Cryptography (ECC)

ECC is an asymmetric encryption method based on the algebraic structure of elliptic curves. It offers high security with smaller key sizes compared to other methods.

Example: ECC is widely used in mobile devices due to its efficiency and lower computational cost.

Learn More

Transposition Cipher

This cipher rearranges the characters of the plaintext message. Unlike substitution ciphers, the letters are moved, not substituted.

Example: "HELLO" transposed with a 3x3 grid might become "LHELO".

Learn More

One-Time Pad

The One-Time Pad is an unbreakable encryption method that uses a random key as long as the plaintext message. The key is discarded after use.

Example: "HELLO" encrypted with a random key could become "XJWEZ".

Learn More

Playfair Cipher

The Playfair Cipher is a digraph substitution cipher that encrypts pairs of letters based on a 5x5 letter grid.

Example: The pair "HE" from "HELLO" may become "DI" depending on the grid setup.
